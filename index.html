Biography

Cong Zhang is a PhD candidate at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, under the supervision of Prof. George M. Constantinides, Prof. Lars P. Hansen, Prof. Chad Syverson, and Prof. Jeffrey R. Russell.

During his PhD journey, he has been fortunate to be the first PhD candidate from Chicago Booth to earn a Master of Legal Studies degree from the University of Chicago Law School and also completed his MBA degree from the University of Chicago Booth School of Business. He earned the Bachelor's degree with a triple major in Mathematics, honors Economics, Statistics from the University of Michigan.

Cong Zhang's research intersects empirical finance and financial regulation, focusing on how regulatory frictions and economic uncertainties affect asset pricing. His studies at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business and Law School have shaped his approach, providing a strong foundation in both financial and legal principles. This unique blend allows him to tackle complex challenges at the intersection of financial markets and regulatory environments.

Cong's ultimate goal is to refine risk pricing models by incorporating uncertainties like model ambiguity and mis-specification, particularly those related to legal and regulatory changes. By improving the robustness of these models, he aims to create more stable and efficient financial markets. Additionally, his work seeks to bridge the gap between market anomalies and rational mechanisms by rethinking how different uncertainties, especially those caused by regulatory shifts, influence these models.

RESEARCH

JOB MARKET PAPER

Regulatory Uncertainty Pricing in Digital Economy

Abstract: This study delves into the relationship between data-induced market concentration rise and total factor productivity (TFP) growth through the lens of the digital economy. We aim to evaluate the extent to which regulatory uncertainties are priced, by incorporating the non-rivalry and externalities triggered by digital capital while embracing various forms of regulatory changes including data privacy laws and command-and-control regulations. We employ a novel Bayesian methodology developed by Gao and Zhang (2024) to incorporate potential non-stationarities in the risk prices associated with rising market concentration. Our findings reveal a consistent annual negative premium of 3.5% since 2004, coinciding with the growth of data-driven markets. Additionally, the study examines the pronounced effects of regulatory modifications on asset pricing. In particular, we demonstrate how the dynamics of risk pricing can be rationalized collectively by data privacy law and command-and-control regulations.
